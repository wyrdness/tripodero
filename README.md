## Overview

The Tripodero is a tall-tale beast first described in William T. Cox's Fearsome Creatures of the Lumberwoods, With a Few Desert and Mountain Beasts (Judd & Detweiler, Washington, D.C., 1910), illustrated by Coert Du Bois with mock-Latin classifications by U.S. Forest Service dendrologist George B. Sudworth. According to Cox, the Tripodero of the Californian chaparral and foothills 'defies all scientific attempts at classification'. Its small but solidly built body stands on two telescopic legs balanced behind by a kangaroo-like tail, and its head is described as 'nearly all snout'. It hunts birds and small animals by extending its legs to raise itself above the brush, then firing a clay pellet (stored in its left cheek) from its blowgun-like snout. Like other fearsome critters, the Tripodero was never advanced as a real animal but as bunkhouse comedy and as a hazing device for greenhorn loggers.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Cox (1910); a mock-zoological neologism evoking 'tripod' and Latinate species naming.
- **Meaning**: Three-legged shooter (referencing two telescopic legs and balancing tail)
- **First use**: 1910, in Cox's Fearsome Creatures of the Lumberwoods
